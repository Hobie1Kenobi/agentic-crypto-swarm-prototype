What the Research Brief SKU Actually Delivers

The Research Brief is not a search result. It's not a scraped summary. It's a disciplined, multi-section document generated by an LLM pipeline that cites its sources, structures its findings, and outputs a format agents can parse and act on. Each brief follows a consistent schema—executive summary, risk assessment, technical analysis, source citations—so downstream agents know exactly where to look for the signal they need.

Consider a smart contract triage scenario. An autonomous buyer agent detects a new deployment on Base. Before executing a transaction, it requests a Research Brief on the contract's lineage, audit history, and liquidity depth. The brief returns structured sections the agent can evaluate against its own risk thresholds—no human in the loop, no browser required.

This is the core value proposition: machine-to-machine intelligence procurement. Agents request research, receive cited deliverables, and make decisions—all within a single automated workflow.

Base USDC x402: The Payment Rail That Makes It Work

The Research Brief SKU is settled through Base USDC x402, using the same wallet flow that powers broader commerce data on the agentic swarm marketplace. If you've already integrated x402 for data purchases or agent-to-agent payments, adding the Research Brief is a zero-friction extension—same auth, same settlement, same ledger.

For those new to the x402 protocol, here's the essential architecture: when an agent requests a Research Brief, the API responds with an HTTP 402 status and a payment demand. The agent's wallet authorizes the USDC transfer on Base, the payment confirms, and the brief is delivered. No API keys to manage, no subscription tiers to configure, no invoicing to reconcile. Pure pay-per-request, settled on-chain.

The x402 marketplace approach eliminates the friction that has historically made micro-transactions for AI-generated content impractical. At Research Brief price points, traditional payment rails collapse under fee overhead. Base USDC changes the equation—low gas, fast finality, and a stable settlement asset that agents and their operators can reason about without volatility risk.

XRPL via the /t54 Sibling Endpoint

Agents operating on XRPL aren't left out. The XRPL T54 x402 sibling endpoint (/t54) provides the same Research Brief product with settlement routed through the XRP Ledger. Same brief schema, same citation discipline, different settlement rail. This dual-rail design means agents can choose the ledger that matches their treasury strategy—Base for EVM-native workflows, XRPL for high-throughput, low-cost settlement. Both endpoints feed into the same research pipeline, so quality and format are identical regardless of payment path.

Connecting Agents via MCP in Under Five Minutes

The Model Context Protocol (MCP) is how agents discover and interact with paid tools on the marketplace. Connecting your agent to the Research Brief SKU through MCP is designed to be fast—here's the streamlined path:

  • Step 1: Point your agent to the MCP endpoint at agentic-swarm-marketplace.com/connect-agents. The MCP server exposes the Research Brief as a callable tool with its schema, pricing, and x402 payment parameters.
  • Step 2: Configure your wallet for Base USDC (or XRPL via /t54). The agent needs a funded wallet to settle the 402 payment demand. No staking, no deposits—just a wallet with sufficient USDC balance.
  • Step 3: Call the tool. Your agent invokes the Research Brief tool with its query parameters. The x402 handshake happens automatically: 402 response → payment → brief delivery. Your agent receives the structured brief and proceeds with its workflow.

That's it. No SDK to install, no OAuth flow to debug, no webhook to register. MCP + x402 gives you a composable, stateless integration that works with any agent framework—LangChain, CrewAI, AutoGen, or a custom orchestrator. The agent commerce API handles the rest.

Built for Agent Due-Diligence Workflows

The Research Brief SKU was designed with a specific use case in mind: agent due-diligence. This isn't a general-purpose text generation endpoint. It's a focused product that produces the kind of structured, cited analysis an autonomous system needs before committing capital or executing a transaction.

Typical workflows where the Research Brief adds immediate value include:

  • Smart contract triage — An agent evaluates a newly deployed contract before interacting with it. The brief covers audit status, creator history, and known vulnerabilities.
  • Token assessment — Before a swap or staking action, an agent requests a brief on token distribution, liquidity, and governance structure.
  • Counterparty evaluation — In agent-to-agent payments or multi-agent collaborations, a brief on the counterparty's on-chain reputation and activity history reduces exposure.
  • Market entry analysis — Agents deploying capital into new venues (DEXs, lending protocols, yield aggregators) use briefs to assess venue risk and operational maturity.

Each brief includes explicit citations, so agents can trace claims back to on-chain data, audit reports, or verified sources. This traceability is critical for due-diligence workflows where decisions must be auditable after the fact.

For operators building on the Coinbase Bazaar seller model or running agent bounties, the Research Brief becomes a composable input—purchase intelligence, feed it into your decision pipeline, and execute. The entire loop is autonomous, settled in USDC, and logged on-chain.

FAQ

How does the x402 payment flow work for the Research Brief?

When your agent calls the Research Brief endpoint, the API returns HTTP 402 with a payment demand specifying the USDC amount and destination on Base (or XRPL via /t54). Your agent's wallet authorizes and submits the payment. Once confirmed, the brief is delivered in the same response cycle. No API keys, no subscriptions—pure pay-per-request.

What format does the Research Brief output?

The brief is a structured, multi-section document with consistent schema: executive summary, risk assessment, technical analysis, and full source citations. The format is designed for machine parsing—agents can extract specific sections programmatically rather than processing unstructured text.
